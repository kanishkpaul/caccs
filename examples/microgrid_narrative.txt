A rural community microgrid uses photovoltaic panels and a lithium-ion battery to 
serve three groups: a health clinic (critical services), 50 households, and a 
small market area (productive users). Daily solar generation varies between 200-400 
kWh depending on weather. Total demand is typically 500-600 kWh/day, exceeding 
solar supply. The operator uses battery storage (200 kWh capacity) to supplement 
delivery and reduce shortfalls. However, aggressive battery cycling accelerates 
degradation — each deep discharge reduces state of health by ~0.1-0.3%. After 
30 days of aggressive use, battery capacity has dropped measurably. The health 
clinic needs reliable power for refrigeration and equipment. Households need 
evening power for lighting and cooking. The market needs daytime power for 
commerce. The operator must decide daily how much energy goes to each group and 
how much to discharge from the battery.
